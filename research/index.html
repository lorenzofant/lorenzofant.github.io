Research

Microbial evolution under extreme starvation: Most microorganisms on Earth exist in a state of severe starvation and/or energy limitation. These harsh environmental conditions place severe physiological stressors on microbial growth and reproduction. We know very little about whether microorganisms can adapt to such conditions. In fact, average generation time can increase to the point that there is uncertainty about whether evolution (i.e., the change in allele frequencies over multiple generations) can occur on a geologic time-scale. To determine how microorganisms evolve under the ecologically-common state of severe starvation I have been running a long-term evolution experiment using a set of phylogenetically-diverse bacteria with the help of research technician Evgeniya Polezhaeva. Using this system we will be able to determine the rate of evolution as well as to what degree parallel molecular and trait evolution occurs among populations within and between highly divergent taxa.

The evolutionary implications of dormancy: Depending on the environment, > 90% of microorganisms may be metabolically inactive (i.e., dormant) and persist in a reservoir of genetic diversity known as the seed bank. The amount of time that microorganisms can spend in the seed bank is highly variable, ranging from a few days to, in the very extreme, hundreds of millions of years. This ability to survive and persist for extended periods of time means that the length of time that a cell can remain dormant far exceeds the average generation time in an optimal environment. Active individuals can continue to compete and allele frequencies will continue to change for a portion of the population, but any change in genetic diversity will be buffered by the presence of dormant individuals. Currently I am using population genetic theory to examine how the presence of a seed bank alters the evolutionary dynamics of microorganisms.

Microbial biodiversity theory: While microbes are the most abundant and taxonomically diverse group of organisms on the planet, relatively little effort has been given towards testing whether theories of biodiversity explain patterns of microbial commonness and rarity. In a recent collaboration with Kenneth J. Locey and my advisor, Jay T. Lennon, we tested theories of biodiversity on a compilation of > 20,000 microbial community datasets. We found that microbial communities most often follow lognormal dynamics (i.e., the synergistic interactions of stochastic processes) and that communities of larger size are more likely to follow lognormal dynamics. This work can be found in Nature Ecology and Evolution

To follow-up on this work, I am aiming to extend the results on testing macroecological models of taxonomic diversity on microbial communities presented here to test evolutionary models of genetic diversity on microbial populations. If you’re interested in this work, or microbial macroecology in general, check out Ken’s publications.
